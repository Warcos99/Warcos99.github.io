### 1. Setting up the problem

The world we inhabit is one that is increasingly polarized and politicized.   One of the unfortunate side effects here is that it's becoming harder to keep our sights on what to believe.  We can get in the weeds about the nature of truth and epistemology, but my interests in developing this theory of tensions is to facilitate understanding the mechanisms by which we believe what we do, and how those beliefs interact with each other. Going through the forest of knowledge with epistemology as your guide will certainly help you understand how it is that this forest came about, the composition of the soils and the plants, it will elucidate why certain behaviors are present among the wildlife, but it will not help you find your way around and out of the forest into shelter.  

And before we get too far here, I want to clearly and flatly express some truths that are not being called in to question.  The following statements are true in so far as there exists mountains of evidence to support them:
1. Systemic Racism is real, and shows it's ugly head in many different forms.
2. Queer people are marginalized and driven to dangerous situations for exercising their freedom to be.
3. Patriarchal structures have pushed women down for generations. 
4. The Industrial revolution and the advent of mass market manufacturing is putting a great strain on the planet, from the creation of greenhouse gasses to the accumulation of too much trash for us to handle.  
5. Late stage capitalism is grinding the common man to dust for the benefit of the elite few.

If you have issue with any of these statements, you probably actually have an issue with the secondary conclusions one might draw from these statements (or you just refuse to see the truth).  You might see the strain we are putting on the planet and draw one of several conclusions.  Some popular examples of conclusions others have draw are:
- we need to take better care of the planet in every respect, including personal responsibility to up-cycle and recycle
- we need to hold companies accountable for the mess they are creating, and we need to add legislature to control and fix how these companies pollute. Asking regular people to bear the weight of the problem is a tactic used by these companies to remove responsibility from themselves
- relying on governments to solve this problem is a fools errand, we need to think of more radical solutions, since corporations cannot be trusted to do the right thing, politicians cannot be trusted to not be bought.
- I dunno, maybe you believe that the incoming environmental disaster we are creating is just the next chapter in the story of the planet.  And we will have to contend with those consequences, and our energies are better spent in preparing for that
- and like five hundred other beliefs.

One last disclaimer here is that I do not plan on telling you what to believe.  I have personal believes that are resulting from this way of thinking about ideas, and as I explain the process I will use my beliefs as examples.  But the intention is not to convince anyone of anything.  I am not above propaganda as a tool for changing public opinion, but this is not meant for that. Instead I just intend to explain my system of characterizing all the noise around us into sensible strings of thought.

### 2. A taught string tied on both ends.

First, imagine a viewpoint that leaves no room discussion. I will use the following example: Someone comes up to you and says *"all items in category A are bad"*.  When I suspect a statement like this is being made, I like to imagine it as a node in space.  I tie a string to it and let the rope dangle.  Then I think about the extremity of the statement and try to imagine it's opposite. One version of that for this particular example would be *"All items in category A are good"*.  I imagine that as a different node, and tie the other end of the string to it.  The string will sit mostly taught between both nodes.  Lets define a point that I will call the "apex" of the string.  The apex will be defined as the point of the string that is feeling the highest tension between both nodes.  Imagine a zooming in on the apex, and seeing the neighboring points, points to the left of the apex will feel a strong tension force to the left node, and points to the right of the apex will feel a strong tension force to the right of the node. The point at the apex itself is feeling a net force of zero because the tension force on the left will exactly cancel out with the tension force on the right.

In this thought experiment, the string is probably obeying the laws of physics in your head, so the apex is probably smack in the middle.  But imagine a more complex theoretical space with me.  In this mind-space, each node comes with it's own "mass". Imagine these nodes exist in a zero gravity environment, where the the nodes themselves have a mass and exert some gravitational field of their own.  The result here would be the that rope would not simply apex in the midpoint of the nodes.  The rope would flex and wane with respect to the gravitational field created by these two nodes.  The apex of the string in this situation would be determined by how large the gravitational potential of the two competing nodes would be. This apex is the point of "truth"  

Importantly, the mass or weight of the nodes is determined by your underlying philosophies, and so the beliefs you accept as truths will be determined by those philosophies.

Let's do a quick example.
The statement *violence is bad for societal progress* is presented. So we compare that to a diametrically opposed statement. We come up with *violence is necessary for societal progress*  
Imagining both of those statements as nodes in space, we tie a string to both nodes.  Depending on your personal believes, historical data, and any other relevant points of information, you can start to imagine how that string is going to react when placed between those two nodes.   For the sake of example, I will use some of my beliefs, but again the point here is not to convince you to believe what I do, but rather to see how the mind-frame works.

What are the factors that influence the weight of each node for me?

well, for *"violence is bad for societal progress"* I have
- guilt about hurting others
- fear of violence being done unto me
- a strong desire to resolve conflicts non-violently
- certain examples of non-violent revolutions and movements
	- You know, Martin Luther King Jr, Ghandi, etc...
- certain examples of violent revolutions failing, or succeeding at changing the status quo at the cost of human suffering and instilling new emperors
	- French revolution, American Revolution, Russian Revolution, etc...

for *"violence is necessary for societal progress"*
- frustration with how slow change is supposed to happen in this country
- fear of violence being done unto me by the state
- realizing that non-violent methods are not effective against capitalists who will take your revolution and sell it back to you for their own profit.
- Examples of non-violent movements have a backbone of violence.
	- For every Martin Luther King Jr, there is a Malcolm X.
- The true monarchy killer ended up being the world-wars, famously and terribly violent affairs
- A belief that violence is a word used by the state to sanction certain behaviors as "ugly" and re-brand their own similar behaviors as "justice"
- A pretty wide definition of violence
	- Physical violence onto people, destruction of property, rhetorical violence, etc...  I think a lot of things that one might not consider are actually examples of violence.

Where does that leave things for me?  well, it leaves the apex of the string somewhere in the middle, but favoring the "violence is necessary side".  What that ends up meaning for me is that certain types of violence are absolutely necessary (particularly rhetorical violence). On the other hand, other forms of violence, I cannot currently condone under my belief system (namely the injuring of other humans). As such, I don't believe I can be a pacifist, but I also don't believe I can ever injure someone else. This is a truth I have found for myself, and this truth may be different for you.

### 3. A note on complexity

One neat thing about this system of visualizing arguments is that it allows for a neat trick when considering more complex situations.  On it's face, this technique does not seem much different than simply considering all your knowledge about a topic before making a decision about what your belief in that topic is. But it is easy to imagine a situation where discerning your belief about a topic is determined by your belief in another topic, and so on.  In this situation, if one wishes to understand why they believe what they do, then they would have to go on this long winding road of understanding how different believes are dependently built on one another.  

Visualizing your beliefs as these strings on nodes, it's easy to imagine what this means for dependent beliefs.  A dependent belief is one that  has it's own two nodes in mind-space, and whose string is tangled in with the one you are observing.  From this viewpoint, you can imagine the placement of the nodes matters, as well as the way in which the strings pull on each other.  for example, in my brain, the nodes of extreme violence and extreme non-violence are on one axis, while the nodes of "make your art subtle" and "make your art obnoxious" are on an orthogonal axis.  Meaning that when I consider how the string of violence and the string of art are tangled together, I can imagine as those two strings pulling on each other in directions not commensurate with their nodes, causing incredible tension.  And that ends up being a true reflection of my feelings on violence and art.  Art without teeth is bland, but art that is needlessly violent is provocative in ways that is not useful and often times just masturbatory. 

Realizing that your belief system is just a bunch of nodes and strings, and that each string is pulling on each other in interesting and unique ways is a layer of complexity that is allowable by this frame of mind.  It makes parsing complicated relationships between already complicated ideas easier to understand.  

### 4. A more grounded example

Alot of what I have outlined is meant to help parse the complex questions of the bigger world. Violence, Art, Truth, etc... This frame of mind is helpful for understanding your place in that world, but it is also very handy for understanding an equally complicated field: social interactions.  

I find that alot of social tension stems from two parties believing something that is not commensurate with each other.  A simple example, I may believe that I am fun to watch movies with, because I crack good jokes. My wife on the other hand may believe I am terrible to watch movies with because I don't shut up. (Don't worry, I don't actually talk during movies).  If I am having a discussion (or god forbid, an argument) with her about this, I may have this node of "i'm worth listening to" in my head.  When she tells me "you talk to much during movies" I may instinctively feel defensive and want to put the words in her mouth: "You are not worth listening too".  But that is not what she is saying, so I take a step back, and make a node in my head for "you talk to much during movies".  Then I put the string in between them.  As I measure the gravity of each node, I start to consider what she might mean, i start to consider that she might have grown up in a different manner than me, where wise-cracking was not as welcome in her house as it was in mine.  I also consider why I feel defensive about her statement, and why it is that I want to be heard.  The whole ordeal becomes a very introspective journey, but culminates in the realization that what I value and what she values in a movie watching experience are different things. 

I may come to a realization that i may have to compromise my wise-cracking a little bit for her enjoyment.  Or If the situation is reversed and she is the talker, I may come to the compromise of pausing the movie whenever she has something to say, so I can give her the attention she desires.

either way, putting the string in between the nodes, and evaluating the nodes, understanding where that string is getting tangled with other strings, and understanding where the truth lies in all of that is an important process in the journey for truth, and in social settings, the journey for "getting out of my own head, and existing in a space where other people also exist".

It should go without saying, but this particular example is not a real one, though this frame of mind has helped me to understand my place in social settings in very similar ways.
